# Category Context: Espresso Machines

Subcategory of: `kitchen-appliances`

## Key Buying Criteria

### Critical questions to ask in intake
- **Automation level** — this is the single most important question:
  - *Super-automatic*: bean-to-cup fully automated (press button → espresso). Easy, consistent, expensive.
  - *Semi-automatic*: you control the shot (grind, tamp, pull). Most enthusiast machines. Best quality ceiling.
  - *Manual/lever*: full control, high skill floor, niche audience.
  - Ask: "Do you want a machine that does everything automatically, or are you happy learning
    the craft of pulling a shot?"
- **Grinder situation**: Do they already own a grinder? If not, do they want one built-in
  (combo machine) or separate? Separate grinder + machine generally produces better results.
  Never assume they have (or don't have) a grinder.
- **Milk drinks**: Do they make lattes/cappuccinos? If yes, do they want a steam wand (manual frothing)
  or an automatic milk frother? Steam wands make better microfoam but require practice.
- **Warm-up time tolerance**: Prosumer machines can take 20–30 min to reach thermal stability.
  Is a quick warm-up important to them?
- **Counter space**: Measure matters. Some machines (e.g., Rocket, La Marzocco) are large.
  Ask if counter space is limited.
- **Coffee input**: Whole beans (they grind)? Pre-ground? ESE pods? Nespresso-style capsules
  (this is a different product category)?
- **Budget**: Espresso machines span $50 (Moka pot adjacent) to $5,000+ (prosumer). Budget determines
  the entire shortlist. A "flexible" budget needs to be pinned down.
- **Experience level**: Complete beginner → experienced home barista. Shapes how forgiving a
  machine they need.

### Key specs to include in comparison table
- Machine type (super-auto / semi-auto / manual)
- Pump pressure (bars) — 9 bar is ideal for espresso; more is not better
- Boiler type: single boiler, heat exchanger (HX), or dual boiler
  (dual boiler = best for simultaneous brew + steam)
- Warm-up time
- Built-in grinder? (yes/no, grinder quality if yes)
- Steam wand: manual / automatic / none
- Water reservoir size (L)
- Price (new)

### Common pitfalls
- Budget machines (under $200) often can't maintain consistent 9-bar pressure or temperature —
  results will always be inconsistent, no matter the skill.
- "I'll get a Nespresso" is not an espresso machine recommendation — it's a pod system.
  If the user wants true espresso, guide them away from pod-only machines.
- Machines with pressurized portafilters (most entry-level): they're more forgiving but
  cap the quality ceiling. Worth flagging.
- The grinder matters as much as the machine. A cheap grinder + great machine = mediocre espresso.
  If they have no grinder budget, factor that in.
- Scale creep: enthusiasts often end up upgrading within 1–2 years. If the user seems
  genuinely passionate, suggest skipping the entry tier.
- Descaling requirements vary significantly — some machines need it every 2–3 months with hard water.

### Price tiers (as of 2024–2025)
- Entry: $150–$400 (Breville Bambino, DeLonghi Dedica) — pressurized portafilter, limited control
- Mid: $400–$800 (Gaggia Classic Pro, Breville Barista Express) — real espresso quality, learning curve
- Enthusiast: $800–$1,500 (Lelit Mara, Rancilio Silvia Pro) — HX or dual boiler, prosumer feel
- Prosumer: $1,500–$3,000+ (Lelit Bianca, ECM Synchronika) — commercial-grade home machines
